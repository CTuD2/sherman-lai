Profile

A career built on two disciplines

Sherman Lai has practised Traditional Chinese Medicine and acupuncture in Guelph since 1986, and has taught Ninjutsu — a martial art passed through 27 generations of his family — at the University of Guelph since 1983.

Lai is the founder and director of the Oriental Healing Arts Research Institute and the director of the Centre of Integrative Natural Medicine, a clinic that has treated more than 20,000 patients from across Canada and internationally. The clinic is known for an integrative approach that combines Chinese medicine with Western science, nutritional science, and lifestyle coaching, treating chronic and acute pain, digestive disorders, arthritis, sleep disorders, and hormonal imbalances, among other conditions.

In 2002, Lai founded BioHerbalai Inc. to bring together classical Chinese herbal medicine and contemporary bio-science, manufacturing herbal formulations and providing education for health practitioners. In 2003, he was appointed by the Ontario Minister of Health to the advisory committee that helped establish the regulatory framework for Traditional Chinese Medicine and acupuncture in the province — work that contributed to Ontario becoming one of the first provinces in Canada to formally regulate the professions.

"Nothing speaks louder than clinical results. It's very effective for patients suffering from chronic pain, injury or accidents... the basic principle is to heal the body without being invasive." — Sherman Lai, Guelph Mercury, October 2003

Alongside his clinical work, Lai has spent decades in community and international service. He has been a member of Rotary International since 1992, chaired the Rotary Club of Guelph's Health Committee, and supported hospital and children's fundraising throughout the region. In 1998 he volunteered for three weeks in Taiwan providing medical care to Indigenous communities living in poverty, a relationship that continues today through his role as a medical advisor to St. Mary's Hospital in Tai-Tung, one of the most remote hospitals in Taiwan.
